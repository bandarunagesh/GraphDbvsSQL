Basic Rules
1. Primary brand colors: Company Soft White & Warm Black
﻿Our primary colors must always be visible and predominant in initial contact with our brand. Company Soft White is primarily used for backgrounds and Company Warm Black for typography and our logo.  

2. Accent color: Company Space Orange
﻿Use Space Orange for bold highlights and key interactive elements. This includes the "Input" line, as a disruptor color, and for elements like buttons and links. Learn more about the "Input" line in layout principles. When used for text, orange must be larger than 19 pt or 24 px.

3. Gradient: Company Space Gradient 
﻿The gradient can complement our brand but not dominate it. It is used over a large area in certain defined layouts. You can find out which layouts are suitable for gradients in the layout principles. 


Company grid
Flexible yet unique - the holistic layout system of Company.

In the layout system, all our key assets come together to form the new brand. From logo to font and color, to our imagery and illustrations – every component aligns to create Company in a modern, sophisticated, and flexible way. A system that connects science and humanity in every expression.

Grid structure
To ensure a consistent and user-friendly application, we have defined a simple method for applying the Company grid across all digital and print formats. The following section explains and demonstrates these steps.

Step 01: Identify the margin
The first step is to define the margin – the space between the grid and the edge of the format. Establishing this margin is essential, as it creates a clear visual framework that ensures readability, balance, and harmony within the design. By maintaining consistent spacing, the layout remains open and breathable, preventing overcrowding and allowing each element to stand out with clarity and purpose.

Step 02: Create the grid
The next step is to establish the actual grid system. This system is built on mathematical principles to ensure maximum flexibility, consistency, and ease of use across all applications. It is important to note that the grid is designed around standard formats, providing a reliable foundation for most layouts. In cases involving extreme or unconventional formats, slight adjustments may be required. These special cases are outlined in the following section.

For this step we divide the short side of the format into 6, the longer side into 12 Units.

Step 03: Spacing
The space between units is called the gutter. It plays a key role in maintaining readability and visual harmony. To ensure consistency, the gutter spacing is once again defined by the short side of the format.

Basic layout structures
Four layout structures create consistency and flexibility across formats.

Basic layout structure A: Headline and supporting type, paired with two images,  either gradient and imagery, or human and science imagery combined.

Basic layout structure B: Same as structure A, with visuals anchored at the top. Type volume adjusts based on image size.

Basic layout structure C: Headline and supporting type, combined with one big hero image, either place on the bottom, top, right or left.

Basic layout structure D: No image, just h and supporting type and on a white or gradient background.

Basic rule: When using two or more images, the larger image anchors to two sides of the format; the smaller image anchors to at least one side. When using a single image, it must anchor to three sides to ensure a strong, stable composition.

Layout structure A:
Components: Logo, headline, and supporting type at top; two visual elements below.

Description: This layout pairs text with two images, offering flexible combinations based on content needs:

Human + science – one image shows a human imagery, the other represents science. Placement is flexible
Human + gradient – a human image paired with a gradient to create balance or draw focus
Science + gradient – a science image paired with a gradient to create balance or highlight the content
Flexible pairing allows adaptation by message, audience, and format while maintaining visual consistency. Ideal when text and visuals carry equal weight.

sLogo centred.

Components: Two visual elements at top; logo, headline and supporting type below.

Description: This layout leads with visuals, placing images above text. Uses the same flexible pairings as structure A:

Human + science – one human element, one science element
Human + gradient – human image with gradient for balance or emphasis
Science + gradient – science image with gradient for balance or focus
The amount of type may vary depending on the size and prominence of the visuals.Ideal for attention-grabbing formats, such as posters, banners, or digital ads, where the image is the first element the viewer notices.

Layout structure C:

Description: One dominant visual – image or gradient – positioned bottom, top, right or left. Text can be placed above, below or next to the image.

Best for presentations, hero visuals, and high-impact marketing materials.

Visual not touching sides.

Layout structure D:
Components:  Text only, with a white or gradient background.

Description: A text-focused layout for when imagery would distract. Use gradients to add depth and highlight content areas, creating visual interest without images.Ideal for internal communications, instructional materials, or simple flyers.

Dont’s:
White type on gradient.
Text that does not follow the defined typographic rules.
Gradient not touching sides.

Multiple Images
For non-hero applications
Components: Introduction, and multiple non-hero images.

Description: This layout option is for applications where multiple images are required. However, it should not be used for feature intros or hero-sections such as homepage banners, website section chapters, posters or covers (in these cases please use layout structures A–D). The multiple image layout option pairs text with three to four images, offering flexible combinations based on content needs. A recommended limit of four images per canvas/layout should be observed. Avoid including too many images on a single page. Only use images that meaningfully support the message or content. Do not use gradients or the Input in multiple-image layouts.

Flexible pairing allows adaptation by message, audience, and format while maintaining visual consistency. Ideal when text and visuals carry equal weight.

Dont’s:
Ensure that at least one image touches two or more edges of the frame.
Avoid leaving empty space in the center when using multiple images.
Do not break up images with text or other content; maintain them side by side.
Do not use an Input on layout structure E.

Gradient placement
Space Orange follows the Input
Always position the gradient so the Company Space Orange section aligns with the direction of the Input.
Gradient Usage
The Company brand includes multiple gradient variants, including non-linear Gradient Variants 01, 02, and 03, as well as a linear gradient.

Across brand design applications, all gradient variants may be used interchangeably depending on context and creative intent. Within infographics, Gradient Variant 01 is the primary gradient. The linear gradient may be used selectively for thin bars or similar functional elements where a non-linear gradient is not suitable.

Gradients should always support content and must not dominate the overall visual impression.

Accessibility
Gradients should be used with care and only where all color transitions maintain sufficient contrast to meet accessibility requirements. Gradients must not be introduced in functional or informational assets such as icons, QR codes, charts, or UI components. In particular, QR codes must always be solid, high-contrast, and free of gradients to ensure reliable scannability and usability across devices and lighting conditions.

Typography
Timeless type, sharpened for today.

We’ve retained Volta, honoring our Swiss lineage. Derived from Neue Haas Grotesk, Volta Modern communicates confidence, precision, and assurance with effortless legibility. Gentle curves meet clean lines – technical clarity with human warmth. Two weights. Maximum impact. Pure Swiss design used in the every day.

Basic rules:
1. Font usage: Volta Modern Display  
﻿Use Volta Modern Display only. Headlines in Medium, Subheadlines in Roman with Medium highlights, Body Copy in Roman with Medium highlights.

2. Color usage
﻿Use Company Warm Black or Soft White for typography. Space Orange may be applied sparingly for highlights or small details and must not be smaller than 19 pt / 24px. White text on an orange background must also meet this minimum size for accessibility reasons.

3. Text placement
﻿Follow the grid system for all text placement. Maintain clear hierarchy, consistent spacing, and alignment across layouts.

The use of typography requires reoccurring rules to create a holistic visual identity that is unmistakably Company. The following points show how these rules creates an impactful design component within the layout grid.

Font styles
The Volta Modern Display typeface offers a wide range of font styles, from Thin to Black. This flexibility provides great freedom in design but also carries the risk of overcomplicating the visual system. To maintain a holistic and distinct appearance, we defined two primary styles for Company: Roman and Medium. Limiting the font to these two ensures a user-friendly system that consistently aligns with the Company brand.
﻿The Volta Modern Display typeface is now the only font used across all Company communications. Volta Modern Text is no longer applied.

Font highlights in Company Space Orange
Orange may be used to highlight key elements in text. When using orange on a soft white background, the text must have a minimum size of 19 pt (Medium) and 29 pt (Roman) to ensure legibility. Orange highlights are limited to accent elements only (e.g., short headlines, markers, call-outs, emphasis lines), and must not be used for long or large text blocks, or text smaller than 19 pt or 24px. 

The purpose of orange is to direct attention, not to define the overall color impression.

Alignment
As a typographic principle, text should be left-aligned across all touchpoints to reflect fundamental Swiss layout principles, giving the brand an overall visual consistency.  Left-aligned text in combination with ragged-right (how the sentences end) supports readability, especially for people with visual or reading impairments. Justified or right-aligned text should not be used.
﻿

Please note: By default, all text is left-aligned. Centered alignment is permitted only for single-word headlines and is generally not recommended to maintain the overall typographic look.

Infographic modules
All infographics use the Bento Box principle, organizing visual elements into modular sections. This modular approach makes elements flexible, structured, and easily adaptable across different formats and layouts. 

The modules mirror the precision and clarity of the Swiss grid, ensuring visual consistency, balance, and alignment throughout all communications. This framework maintains a clean, organized aesthetic while allowing room for creativity and storytelling within each module.

Color of modules
All infographics must be placed on a Grey 2 background and contained within a dedicated module that visually separates the graphic from surrounding content. This framing ensures clarity, structure, and consistent visual hierarchy across applications. Each module must follow the established layout principles for alignment, spacing, and organization.

Padding for modules
The padding for module boxes is based on the gutter of the respective format. The padding (i.e., the distance between the edge of the module and the infographic) must be at least the gutter width. This ensures that infographics are arranged in an airy and visually balanced manner.
